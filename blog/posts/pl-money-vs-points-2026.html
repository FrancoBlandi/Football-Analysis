The Premier League's 20 clubs are not spending the same. Transfermarkt's squad valuations show a range that runs from €1.31 billion (Manchester City) to €252 million (Burnley). That difference reflects years of investment, player development, and wage commitments built into each squad.

But squad value and points are not the same thing. Plotting one against the other for all 20 clubs in 2025/26 reveals which clubs are getting value from their investment and which are wasting it. Two clubs sit more than 15 points below what their squad would predict. Two others sit more than 14 points above. The gaps are large enough that they cannot be explained by variance alone.

The Data

Squad value vs. points: all 20 clubs

Squad market values come from Transfermarkt, the standard public reference for player valuations. The figures reflect the combined estimated market value of each club's registered squad as of May 2026. Points are from the Premier League standings at GW37, with one or two rounds remaining.

How the expected points are calculated: A linear regression is fitted through all 20 clubs, using squad value as the predictor and points as the outcome. The resulting trend line is the expected relationship between investment and results. A club above the line earned more points than their squad value predicts. A club below it earned fewer.

The delta is the gap between actual and expected points. A delta of +15 means a club outperformed their squad value by 15 points. A delta of -18 means the opposite.

Three of the four biggest underperformers have expensive squads: Chelsea (-18.4), Tottenham (-17.8), and Wolves (-19.6). The fourth, Burnley (-16.8), is relegated alongside Wolves, which partly explains their deficit. But Chelsea and Tottenham are not relegated. At the other end, the positive outliers are more spread across the table. Aston Villa (+14.6) and Manchester United (+14.1) both outperform significantly. Below them, Sunderland (+9.0), Arsenal (+9.3), and Bournemouth (+8.9) form a second tier of clubs getting clear value from their squads.

The Stories

Six clubs, six different relationships with their investment

Chelsea: the most expensive mid-table finish in Premier League history. Chelsea's squad is valued at €1.16 billion by Transfermarkt, the third highest in England. That number reflects years of aggressive transfer activity since 2022, including dozens of signings and substantial wage commitments. The return on that investment in 2025/26: 49 points, 10th place, and a deficit of 18.4 points below what a squad of that value would normally produce. No other established club in the sample comes close to that gap. Tottenham, at -17.8, is the nearest comparison, and Tottenham are 17th.

€1.16bn Chelsea's squad value: the third highest in the Premier League. Their league position: 10th. Their delta: -18.4 points.

Tottenham: two years, same problem. Tottenham's €802.5m squad places them 5th by investment among all 20 clubs. Their 38 points at GW37 put them 17th, within sight of a relegation battle they had no business being near. That is a deficit of 17.8 points below expectation. The infrastructure of a top-6 club, the results of a side fighting to stay up. The story is not new, but the data makes it impossible to attribute to bad luck.

Manchester United: the clearest turnaround in the data. United's squad value of €747m ranks 6th among all 20 clubs. Their 68 points and 3rd place finish represents a surplus of 14.1 points above expectation, the second highest overperformance in the sample. At the start of the INEOS era, United were a cautionary tale of mismanagement. These numbers suggest the project is producing results that go beyond what the squad investment alone would predict. It is the most unambiguous positive story in the top half of the table.

+14.1 points above expected for Manchester United: 6th most valuable squad, 3rd in the table.

Aston Villa: the best return on investment in the league. Villa's €547.5m squad ranks 9th by value among the 20 clubs. Their 62 points and 4th place finish represents a surplus of 14.6 points above expectation, the highest in the sample. Villa are above Liverpool (€1.02bn squad, 5th), above Newcastle (€708m, 11th), and within reach of City and Arsenal despite spending a fraction of what those clubs have committed. Their squad value is less than half of Chelsea's. Their table position is six places higher.

+14.6 points above expected for Aston Villa: the highest efficiency rating in the league, with the 9th most valuable squad.

Sunderland: the promoted side that should not be 9th. Sunderland have the 16th most valuable squad in the division at €381.9m, reflecting their recent promotion from the Championship. They sit 9th with 51 points, a surplus of 9.0 points above expectation. For a newly promoted club operating on a fraction of the budgets around them, that result is significant. Sunderland is the data's clearest story of sporting results running ahead of financial scale.

Bournemouth, Brighton, Brentford: the mid-table efficiency cluster. Three clubs with squad values between €470m and €507m all sit 7 to 9 points above expectation. Bournemouth (€507m, 55 pts, +8.9), Brighton (€494m, 53 pts, +7.3), and Brentford (€470m, 52 pts, +7.1) are doing something similar: converting modest squad investment into points at a rate that consistently beats the trend. None of the three have the financial muscle of the top six, but all three have built structures that outperform what their squads are worth.

Conclusions

What the data shows

  • 01 Squad value predicts about half the outcome. The regression explains 48% of the variance in points (R² = 0.48). That is a meaningful relationship: bigger squads do tend to earn more points. But the other 52% reflects factors that money cannot buy: coaching quality, squad cohesion, injury management, and the intangibles of a competitive season.
  • 02 Chelsea's problem is structural, not statistical. A -18.4 point deficit on a €1.16bn squad is not bad luck. It is the result of years of fragmented squad building, high turnover, and a lack of coherent identity on the pitch. The squad is expensive. The system to make it work has not arrived.
  • 03 Aston Villa are the model. The highest efficiency rating in the league (+14.6) with the 9th most valuable squad. Not the biggest budget, not the most famous club. Just the best points-per-euro conversion in the Premier League this season.
  • 04 Mid-table efficiency is clustered and real. Bournemouth, Brighton, Brentford, Fulham, Sunderland, and Leeds all sit 6 to 9 points above expectation. That grouping is not random. All six have clear identities, stable coaching, and squads built with purpose rather than prestige. The table reflects it.
  • 05 Spending more does not protect you. Three of the four biggest underperformers (Chelsea, Tottenham, Wolves) are among the top eight most expensive squads in the league. Large squad values create expectations. When the results do not follow, the gap is proportionally larger.

Methodology and sources

Squad values: Transfermarkt, May 2026. Total squad market value per club, combining all registered first-team players at their current estimated market value. Transfermarkt values reflect player age, form, contract length, and market conditions. They are an estimate of resale value, not a measure of what clubs paid or what they earn. That said, they are the most comprehensive public proxy available for all 20 clubs.

Limitation: Transfermarkt values are updated throughout the season and therefore reflect developments (injuries, form changes, transfers) that occurred during 2025/26 itself. A more rigorous analysis would use pre-season valuations as the predictor. The directional findings are robust either way, but specific delta values should be treated as indicative rather than precise.

Table data: Premier League 2025/26 standings at GW37, mid-May 2026, with one to two rounds remaining. Not all clubs had played the same number of matches at the snapshot date (some clubs at GW36, most at GW37).

Trend line: Ordinary least squares linear regression, 20 observations. Squad value as predictor, points as outcome. Slope: 0.033 points per €1m in squad value. Intercept: 29.5 points. R² = 0.48.

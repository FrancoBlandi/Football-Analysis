Club Atlético Vélez Sársfield — Executive Report

Board-level analysis · Season 2025/26 · Generated 19/04/2026

Section 1 — Structural Deficit in Context

The ARS 16.517B Operating Deficit Is Not a Temporary Dip — It Is Structural

In Fiscal Year 114 (July 2023 – June 2024), Vélez generated ARS 46.598B in ordinary revenues against ARS 63.116B in ordinary expenses. The gap: ARS 16.517B — 35% of revenues consumed by the deficit before a single peso of debt service. The club bridged this with ARS 15.404B in extraordinary financial results (investment returns, inflation adjustments). Strip those out and the operating model is insolvent on its own terms.

This is not a one-year anomaly. A 35% operating gap — expenses exceeding revenues by more than a third — is a structural condition. It means the club's day-to-day cost base is fundamentally misaligned with its recurring income streams. Every year Vélez operates at this ratio, it destroys equity: approximately ARS 16B of patrimonio neto erodes annually unless extraordinary income intervenes.

Benchmarking sharpens the picture. Racing Club runs a structural operating surplus — revenues cover costs without extraordinary support. Boca Juniors posted a record surplus in its most recent balance. Vélez, by contrast, requires a transfer windfall or exceptional financial result every single year just to stay flat. The question is not whether this is sustainable — it is not — but how many years remain before the deterioration becomes existential.

Scenario modeling: At the current ARS 16.517B annual equity erosion rate, Vélez's ARS 42.625B patrimonio neto has approximately 2.6 years of runway before turning negative — absent any structural correction or significant transfer income. Even one EUR 5M transfer sale (approximately ARS 5-6B at current rates) buys roughly four additional months. This is not a comfortable margin.

Board action: The administration must produce a cost-structure breakdown — specifically, what share of ARS 63.116B in expenses is first-team wages, what is academy infrastructure, and what is institutional overhead. A 20% reduction in controllable costs closes roughly ARS 12B of the gap. That is the conversation the board needs to have with actual line-item data.

Section 2 — Squad Value vs. Financial Reality

EUR 32.225M Squad Value: Healthy in Isolation, Exposed in Context

Vélez's Transfermarkt squad valuation of EUR 32.225M represents a genuine off-balance-sheet asset — one that does not appear in the official balance but backs the club's transfer income capacity. The critical adjustment: EUR 2M of that figure is Lucas Robertone, on loan from UD Almería and not a sellable Vélez asset. Effective owned squad value: EUR 30.225M.

Peer comparison is unforgiving. Boca Juniors carries a EUR 90.1M squad — nearly three times Vélez's firepower. River Plate sits in a similar bracket. This gap is not merely a prestige differential; it determines the ceiling on transfer income, the quality of players available for squad depth, and the club's negotiating leverage in the transfer market.

At a 35% operating deficit, the arithmetic is straightforward: Vélez needs to generate approximately EUR 4–5M annually in net transfer income just to break even at current revenue and cost levels. That is not a trivial requirement. It demands a consistent pipeline of sellable assets — players developed to a standard that European or MLS clubs will pay for, with contracts structured to give Vélez negotiating power.

Transfer income requirement breakdown:

ARS 16.517B deficit ÷ ~1,200 ARS/EUR exchange rate = approximately EUR 13.8M annual gap

This cannot realistically be closed by transfers alone — but EUR 4–5M in annual transfer income (roughly one mid-tier sale per year) materially reduces the extraordinary financial results required to stay solvent.

Implication: The squad must be managed as a financial instrument, not only a sporting resource. Every contract renewal decision has a direct balance sheet consequence.

Board action: Commission an internal audit of the full squad's contract structure: which players have clauses above EUR 3M, which contracts expire in 2026 creating zero-value departure risk, and which academy players are approaching sellability. This is portfolio management.

Section 3 — The Youth Academy as the Only Viable Strategy

The Cantera Is Vélez's Primary Financial Asset — and Its Most Fragile One

The evidence is unambiguous. Five homegrown players exported to Europe and MLS in three years: Máximo Perrone (Man City, EUR 9.3M), Santiago Castro (Bologna, EUR 12.05M), Gianluca Prestianni (Benfica, EUR 9M), Valentín Gómez (Real Betis, EUR 5.3M), Maher Carrizo (Ajax, EUR 6M for 50% — implied full valuation above EUR 12M). Combined: approximately EUR 41M in transfer income from academy-developed players in 36 months. This is not a coincidence — it is a system.

The next generation is in the squad. Tobías Andrada (age 19, EUR 3M — Vélez's most valuable player), Joaquín García (age 24, EUR 2.7M), Aarón Quirós (age 24, EUR 2.2M), Jano Gordon (age 21, EUR 1.2M), Silvero (age 20) — all academy products at various stages of development.

The academy financial model: If Vélez sells two academy players per year at EUR 2–3M each, that generates EUR 4–6M in transfer income annually — precisely the amount needed to cover the operating deficit at current revenue levels. The math closes. But this model has an internal tension:

Sustaining a pipeline that produces EUR 10-12M players requires continuous investment in academy infrastructure — facilities, coaching staff, scouting networks. The operating deficit makes that investment harder to fund each year. This is the central catch-22 of Vélez's financial situation: the asset that solves the deficit requires capital the deficit prevents from accumulating.

The Tomás Carrizo sale to Inter Milan for USD 7M confirms the model works at the upper end. The question is whether the board is treating academy investment as a discretionary cost or as the capital expenditure that drives the club's primary revenue stream.

Board action: Ring-fence academy infrastructure budget from cost-cutting exercises. Immediately extend contracts for Andrada, García, and Gordon with release clauses above EUR 5M, EUR 4M, and EUR 3M respectively. A player of EUR 3M market value with an expiring contract is worth EUR 0 to Vélez.

Section 4 — Membership Pricing at Inflection Point

660% Cuota Increase in 2.3 Years: Vélez Has Already Overshot Inflation

The membership fee rose from ARS 5,000 (January 2023) to ARS 38,000 (April 2025) — a 660% increase over 27 months. Accumulated inflation over the same period: approximately 520%. The cuota has outpaced inflation by roughly 140 percentage points in real terms. This is not a neutral adjustment — it represents a genuine price increase for members, in a context where Argentine household purchasing power has deteriorated sharply.

The scale problem compounds this. Vélez has 33,225 active socios. Boca Juniors has 323,586 — nearly ten times the membership base. Even at ARS 38,000/month, Vélez's theoretical maximum annual membership revenue is ARS 15.102B (33,225 × 38,000 × 12). In practice, accounting for arrears, lower-tier categories and churn, the real figure is materially below that. And ARS 15.102B against an ARS 16.517B operating deficit leaves almost nothing.

Revenue ceiling analysis — memberships:

Doubling active socios from 33,225 to 66,450 (a multi-year institutional undertaking) at current cuota levels adds approximately ARS 15B in annual revenue — enough to theoretically close the operating gap. But doubling membership in a city where Boca and River dominate the fanbase is not a near-term lever.

A 10% churn triggered by pricing pressure (3,322 members leaving) costs approximately ARS 1.5B in annual revenue — equivalent to losing one mid-tier transfer sale. The downside of further increases is asymmetric: the incremental revenue per price hike is small; the cost of member loss is immediate and compounding.

Board action: Freeze cuota increases until monthly churn data is available. Implement real-time member retention monitoring. If net membership is declining, the pricing strategy has already crossed the elasticity threshold — further increases accelerate the problem.

Section 5 — Age Structure Paradox

A Squad Built for Transition — Not Peak Performance

Vélez's squad age distribution reveals a structural tension. Eight players are over 31 — Aliendro (35), Romero (34), Lanzini (33), Baeza (32), Magallán (32), Valdés (32), Montero (31), Elías Gómez (31). Ten players are under 22. These two cohorts together represent 67% of the squad. The middle band — ages 22 to 30, the peak value and peak performance window — appears thin.

This matters financially and sporting. Veterans over 31 carry declining transfer value (approaching zero for most) while consuming wage budget. Players under 22 carry future value but current wage discounts — and often require patience before contributing consistently to results. A squad heavy at both extremes is optimized neither for immediate performance nor for financial efficiency. It is a squad in mid-transition.

If sporting results suffer during this transition — reduced Copa Libertadores or Sudamericana participation, for example — the revenue impact compounds the operating deficit. Television rights and Conmebol distributions are not guaranteed; they are performance-dependent.

The veteran wage drain: Eight players over 31 on professional contracts represent a significant portion of the wage bill with no residual transfer value to offset it. Each veteran who departs frees payroll capacity that can be redirected to either youth development or to acquiring a mid-career player in the EUR 1–2M range who still has upward value trajectory.

Board action: Define a 2026–2028 squad renewal plan. For each of the eight veterans over 31: identify whether a one-year extension is justified by performance and wage level, or whether a replacement from the academy or market is the better allocation of resources. The transition is already underway — managing it deliberately is better than letting contracts expire ad hoc.

Section 6 — Loan Strategy Risk and Urgent Contract Decisions

Six Contracts Expiring December 2026 — EUR 7.35M at Risk of Walking Free

Montero (goalkeeper, EUR 2.5M), Robertone (EUR 2M, on loan from Almería), Pellegrini (EUR 1.5M), Lanzini (EUR 1M), Piñeiro (EUR 0.35M) and Machuca all have contracts expiring December 2026. Combined market value: EUR 7.35M. If the board does not act before the June 2026 transfer window, these players will be inside the final six months of their deals — at which point clubs can negotiate free transfers, agents demand higher signing bonuses, and Vélez's negotiating leverage collapses.

The Robertone situation has an additional layer. He is on loan from UD Almería with a purchase option of approximately EUR 4M — at a time when his Transfermarkt valuation is EUR 2M. The option is priced above market. Exercising it requires committing EUR 4M for an asset worth EUR 2M, with no guarantee of appreciation. Not exercising it means Robertone returns to Almería and Vélez rebuilds that squad position from scratch. A third path exists: if a third club is willing to acquire Robertone directly from Almería for EUR 3–4M, Vélez can potentially negotiate a facilitation fee or a solidarity payment. This requires active management, not passivity.

Board action: Before May 2026, assign each of the six expiring contracts to one of three paths: (1) renewal with extended term and updated release clause, (2) sale in the June 2026 window, or (3) planned non-renewal with replacement search initiated. For Robertone specifically: determine by September 2026 whether to exercise the purchase option, negotiate a modified option structure with Almería, or begin the search for a replacement. Indecision on any of these is the worst outcome.

Robertone and the True Owned Squad Value

Loan players add squad depth but zero balance-sheet value. Robertone contributes EUR 2M to the headline squad valuation figure — which the dashboard adjusts to EUR 30.225M for "Vélez-owned value." Any loan-heavy squad strategy creates an illusion of depth that disappears when loans expire simultaneously. The board should monitor the ratio of owned-contract players to loan players as a structural metric, not just the aggregate squad value.

Jonathan Berón (winger, 23) represents a related issue: four consecutive loan spells in Primera Nacional without establishing himself. At 23, the development thesis is running thin. The decision — reintegrate, sell, or rescind — needs to be made at the June 2026 loan expiry, not deferred again.

Section 7 — Three Structural Solutions: Financial Modeling

Path A — Accelerate Academy Sales

The model is proven. EUR 41M from five homegrown players in three years — an average of EUR 8.2M per player sold, or approximately EUR 13.7M per year in transfer income when it happens. The problem: sales cluster in windows (the "Gran Febrero 2024" phenomenon — five simultaneous sales totaling ~EUR 26M), not distributed annually. This creates revenue volatility: flush years followed by lean ones.

A more deliberate pipeline management approach would attempt to stagger development timelines so that one or two players reach sellability each year rather than five in one window. This requires coordination between the technical secretariat's development timeline and the financial department's cash flow projections.

Revenue projection — annual academy sales:

2 sales per year at EUR 2.5M average = EUR 5M/year → closes approximately 36% of the operating gap

1 sale per year at EUR 5M average = EUR 5M/year → same coverage, higher concentration risk

1 sale per year at EUR 10M+ (Castro/Prestianni tier) = EUR 10M/year → closes ~72% of the gap

Timeline: Andrada (19) is the most likely next EUR 5M+ sale, potentially 2027–2028 if development continues. García and Quirós are in the EUR 2–4M range in the near term.

Path B — Reduce Wage Bill by 20% Through Veteran Departures

If the eight veterans over 31 carry above-average wages (a reasonable assumption for experienced professionals), reducing the roster to their replacements from the academy could free significant payroll. A 20% reduction in the ARS 63.116B expense base = ARS 12.6B in annual savings — nearly eliminating the operating deficit.

Sporting risk analysis: The eight veterans over 31 include the goalkeeper (Montero, EUR 2.5M), a starting midfielder (Lanzini, EUR 1M), and multiple defenders. Replacing all eight simultaneously with academy players would almost certainly damage competitive results in the near term — reducing the probability of Copa Libertadores qualification and associated revenue. The optimal path is a phased 3-year transition: two or three departures per year, with academy replacements absorbing minutes gradually.

EUR savings estimate: If each veteran averages EUR 15,000/month in wages (a conservative estimate for players in this market value range), eight departures frees approximately EUR 1.44M/year in wage costs — modest in EUR terms but significant against ARS operating costs.

Path C — Institutional Revenue: Naming Rights and Commercial Partnerships

Vélez's stadium (Estadio José Amalfitani, capacity 49,540) carries naming rights potential that has not been monetized. In the Argentine market, stadium naming rights deals have ranged from USD 1–3M/year for top-tier clubs. At EUR 1M/year, a 5-year naming rights deal generates EUR 5M — equivalent to one mid-tier transfer sale, recurring annually without depleting the squad.

Revenue ceiling: Argentine football naming rights deals are constrained by the relatively smaller sponsorship market compared to Europe. Realistic ceiling in the current environment: USD 1.5–2M/year (approximately EUR 1.3–1.7M). This covers roughly 10–12% of the operating gap annually — meaningful but not transformative on its own. Combined with a commercial sponsorship overhaul (jersey sponsor, training kit, digital inventory), total commercial revenue uplift of EUR 2–3M/year is achievable without any squad changes.

Timeline: Naming rights negotiations require 6–12 months. This is a 2026 initiative, not an emergency lever — but it compounds positively over time and does not carry the sporting risk of wage cuts or the development timeline uncertainty of academy sales.

Integrated View: All Three Paths Together

Combined annual impact (conservative estimates):

Path A — Academy sales: EUR 4–5M/year

Path B — Wage reduction: EUR 1–2M/year equivalent

Path C — Commercial revenue: EUR 2–3M/year

Total: EUR 7–10M/year — sufficient to close or significantly narrow the ARS 16.517B structural gap, depending on exchange rate conditions.

No single path is sufficient. All three pursued simultaneously, over a 3-year horizon, constitute a credible structural correction. The board needs to own all three tracks simultaneously, not sequentially.

Section 8 — Balance Sheet: Solvency With Illiquidity Risk

Positive Net Worth (ARS 42.625B) — But 76% Locked in Illiquid Assets

Vélez closes Fiscal Year 114 with total assets of ARS 67.571B, liabilities of ARS 24.945B, and net worth of ARS 42.625B. The asset-to-liability ratio of 2.7:1 means the club is solvent — it can cover its obligations. This is a genuine institutional strength and distinguishes Vélez from clubs operating with negative equity.

The composition concern: 76% of assets (approximately ARS 51.6B) are non-current — property, installations, long-term rights. Only ARS 15.961B is current (cash, receivables, short-term instruments). A club with ARS 42B in net worth but ARS 16B in liquid assets is wealthy on paper and cash-constrained in practice. Any unexpected expense — an injury requiring a significant purchase, a legal judgment, a Conmebol fine — hits liquidity directly, not equity.

Board action: Identify underutilized non-current assets that could be monetized without strategic loss — rental of training facilities, commercial concessions at the stadium, exploitation of the Villa Olímpica property. Each ARS 1B converted from illiquid to liquid assets materially improves the club's operational resilience.

Section 9 — Transfer Record 2023–2026: EUR 60M in Three Years

12 Confirmed Sales, ~EUR 60M Total — Vélez Is One of Argentina's Most Productive Exporters

The February 2024 window is the headline: five simultaneous sales — Santiago Castro (EUR 12.05M, Bologna), Gianluca Prestianni (EUR 9M, Benfica), Lautaro López (USD 2.7M, City Torque), Walter Bou (USD 1.53M, Lanús), Mauro Pittán (USD 500K, Unión) — totaling approximately EUR 26M in a single window. That level of coordinated market execution is rare in Argentine football and reflects institutional capability, not luck.

Across 12 sales from January 2023 to April 2026, the evaluation distribution skews strongly positive: the majority of operations rated EXCELLENT or GOOD against Transfermarkt benchmarks. Only three transactions (Bou, Bouzat, Pittán) fall in the AT-VALUE category. None classified as AVOIDABLE. This is a transfer record that a UEFA club would be proud of.

The Maher Carrizo sale to Ajax (EUR 6M for 50%, implying a full valuation above EUR 12M) in 2026 confirms the pipeline remains active and the quality of assets produced is internationally recognized.

Board action: Maintain the institutional knowledge that made this record possible. The technical secretariat's market timing and negotiation capability is itself a competitive asset. Protect it through continuity of key personnel and systematic scouting investment.
